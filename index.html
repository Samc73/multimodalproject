The Equity Lens

Cognitive enhancement doesn't happen in a vacuum. It intersects with systems of privilege and inequality in ways that demand our attention.

Economic Access & Pricing

Street prices for Adderall range from $5-20 per pill on college campuses, with costs reaching $0.50-$1.00 per milligram.2,3 Research shows those with higher earnings have better access to cognitive enhancers, whether through legal prescriptions or illicit routes, creating unfair advantages in academic and professional settings.7

Healthcare Disparities

With insurance, monthly Adderall prescriptions cost $40-72. Without insurance, $200-400.9 Students with good insurance can see psychiatrists for proper diagnosis and monitoring. Others turn to unregulated black market sources, risking counterfeit pills—the DEA reports 7 in 10 seized pills contain lethal doses of fentanyl.8

Competitive Environments

Studies document that cognitive enhancer use is more prevalent in highly competitive academic settings.6 The ongoing ADHD medication shortage (announced October 2022) has created additional pressure, with 97% of pharmacies reporting Adderall shortages, forcing legitimate patients to compete for limited supply.10

Self-Sustaining Inequality

Research shows a cyclical effect: those with higher socioeconomic positions have better access to enhancers, which improve academic performance, supporting their privileged standing—a "self-sustaining circle of advantage."7 Meanwhile, 26% of students with prescriptions report sharing or selling their medication, creating an informal market.5

The Pressure to Perform

In competitive academic environments, students face enormous pressure to succeed. For some, cognitive enhancers seem like the only way to keep up.

But access to these substances—whether legal or illicit—isn't equal. Wealth, healthcare access, and social connections determine who can enhance and who gets left behind.

Consider Your Values

There are no easy answers. This scenario invites you to think through the complexities of cognitive enhancement in context.

The Finals Week Dilemma

It's the night before your hardest final. You've been studying, but you're exhausted and struggling to focus. Your roommate offers you an Adderall pill. You don't have a prescription. You know many of your classmates use these drugs during finals. You're worried about your GPA and whether you can keep your scholarship. What factors would shape your decision?

You're thinking about health and safety:

This is crucial. Without a prescription, you don't know if this dose is safe for you, if you have underlying heart conditions, or how it might interact with other factors. The CDC warns that 7 out of 10 pills seized from the illegal drug market contain a lethal dose of fentanyl.8 But here's the equity issue: students with money can see psychiatrists and get prescriptions with proper dosing and monitoring. Students without access to healthcare take on medical risks that wealthier peers don't face.

You're thinking about fairness and competition:

The fairness question cuts both ways. Is it unfair to enhance your performance artificially? Or is the real unfairness that some students have access to tutors, test prep, unstressed environments, and yes, cognitive enhancers, while others don't? When advantage is already distributed unequally, what does "fair competition" mean? Some students need these medications to function at baseline. Where's the line between treatment and enhancement?

You're thinking about access and inequality:

You're identifying the core equity issue. Pills cost $5-20 each on campus, sometimes more during finals.2,3 They're a privilege, not a level playing field. Wealthy students can afford them; others can't. Wealthy students can also afford doctors who might diagnose ADHD and provide legitimate prescriptions. This creates tiered access: some get nothing, some risk black market pills, some get medical supervision. The enhancement landscape mirrors broader educational inequality.

You're questioning the system itself:

This gets at something deeper. Why do students feel they need chemical assistance to meet academic demands? Is the problem individual choices, or institutional cultures that normalize unsustainable workloads and competitive pressure? When universities create environments where enhancement seems necessary to succeed, they're part of the ethical problem. Different students experience this pressure differently based on their resources, support systems, and stakes.
